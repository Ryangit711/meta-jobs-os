### 5 Interview Q&A

**Q1: "Tell me about a complex integration you led."**
> "At SkyflyMD, we went through a $17M acquisition process. I structured 8 concurrent due diligence workstreams — finance, legal, operations, provider contracts, HR, IT, compliance, and facilities. The challenge was that our 5 clinic groups were running 8 different operational systems on different EHR platforms. I built a consolidation plan that transitioned everything to one unified stack within 90 days post-close. We retained 100% of key talent by designing earnout structures that aligned with their personal incentives, not just company milestones. The integration playbook I created is still being used by the acquiring company today."

**Q2: "How do you align cross-functional teams without direct authority?"**
> "I start with a shared North Star. For our acquisition, I framed everything around a simple question: 'Does this help the business continue operating at full speed on Day 1?' That question aligned finance (who wanted cost synergies), operations (who wanted continuity), and legal (who wanted clean documentation). I designed governance rhythms — weekly workstream standups, bi-weekly steering committee — that gave each function a voice while keeping the train moving. My approach: create structure that respects everyone's expertise but doesn't let anyone hold progress hostage."

**Q3: "How do you handle ambiguity in a new integration?"**
> "I've never had a playbook handed to me. At SkyflyMD, every system I built was from zero — there was no template for scaling from 3 to 70 people across 5 clinic groups. My approach is: map what you know, identify the unknowns, and build a decision tree for each unknown. I break ambiguity into workstreams, assign owners, set checkpoints, and escalate when needed. The key is not waiting for complete clarity — it's moving forward with the best available information and adjusting as you learn more."

**Q4: "Why Indeed?"**
> "Your mission — 'We help people get jobs' — is the kind of North Star most companies only pretend to have. You make decisions based on it. That resonates with me because I've always operated that way: start with the end-user and build everything backward from there. Also, I respect that Indeed is a data-driven company that values simplicity. I've never been the person who makes things more complicated. I build systems that are simple enough that people actually use them, rigorous enough that they produce reliable data, and flexible enough that they adapt as the business changes."

**Q5: "What's your approach to creating integration playbooks?"**
> "I believe playbooks should be living documents, not shelfware. My approach: start with Day 1 readiness — what absolutely must be true for the business to operate without interruption on the first day post-close. Build that checklist first. Then layer Day 100 priorities — synergy targets, systems consolidation, cultural integration. The playbook should answer three questions for every workstream: (1) What does done look like? (2) Who owns it? (3) What's the escalation path if it stalls? I also build in retrospective loops — after every integration milestone, 30 minutes to ask 'What surprised us? What should we do differently next time?' That's how you improve the playbook with every acquisition."
