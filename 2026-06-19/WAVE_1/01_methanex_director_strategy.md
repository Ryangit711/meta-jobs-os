**5 Q&A:**

**Q1: Walk me through how you've led a corporate strategy cycle.**
A: At SkyflyMD, I designed the strategy cycle from scratch. It started with environmental scanning — market trends, competitive landscape, regulatory changes. I'd prepare discussion materials for the ELT offsite defining 4-5 strategic themes. We'd run a 2-day facilitated session where the executive team debated and aligned on priorities. I'd capture the outcomes, write the 3-year strategic plan, and present it to the Board for approval. Then I'd translate the plan into quarterly OKRs with owners, metrics, and milestones. Every quarter, I tracked progress and reported back to the ELT. The cycle ran for 5 consecutive years, and it consistently delivered measurable outcomes.

**Q2: Describe your experience with M&A valuation.**
A: During our $17M exit, I was responsible for the financial workstream. I coordinated with external bankers to build the valuation model — DCF, comparable company analysis, and precedent transactions. I presented valuation analyses to the Board alongside the bankers. Post-acquisition, I managed the earnout calculations and impairment testing for the assets. Outside of the exit, I regularly performed valuation analyses — equipment valuations for tax, impairment tests for underperforming clinic assets, and strategic assessments of acquisition targets during our growth phase.

**Q3: How do you facilitate alignment among senior executives with competing priorities?**
A: I've found that executives disagree on outcomes but agree on data. My approach is to surface the underlying assumptions first — what each executive believes to be true about the market, the organization's capabilities, and the competitive landscape. I build a fact base that either validates or challenges those assumptions. Then I frame the strategic options not as "my proposal vs your proposal" but as "Option A leads to Outcome X, Option B leads to Outcome Y, based on these assumptions." I facilitate the trade-off conversation using the data as the neutral ground. This approach consistently produced alignment within a single offsite.

**Q4: How would you approach the signpost process at Methanex?**
A: I'd start by auditing the current process — understanding what signposts are being tracked, who owns them, how they feed into strategic decisions, and where the gaps are. I'd then work with Marketing & Logistics, Global Gas, and Low Carbon Solutions to validate the signpost database against real market shifts — new plant announcements, policy changes, technology disruptions. I'd build a simple categorization framework: signposts that change our strategy, signposts that adjust our tactics, and signposts to monitor. The output would be a living dashboard that keeps the ELT informed without overwhelming them.

**Q5: Why Methanex, and why now?**
A: Methanex is at an inflection point. The energy transition creates both existential questions and generational opportunities for methanol — as a marine fuel, as a hydrogen carrier, as a chemical feedstock in a decarbonizing world. The new CEO in September, the activist investor engagement, the low-carbon solutions build-out — all of these demand a strategy function that can operate at Board level, facilitate complex trade-offs, and move fast. I've been preparing for exactly this context: I've run strategy cycles, executed M&A, built models, and partnered with CEOs. I want to bring that capability to a global industrial leader at a pivotal moment.
